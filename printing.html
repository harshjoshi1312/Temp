What is 3D Printing?


Bring your Idea to life with 3D Printing Technology


In the 20th century, no other invention affected the mankind more than technology did. With the advent of computers in 1950s and internet in 1990s, the fundamental way of doing things has through massive changes. These technologies made our lives better, opened up new avenues and possibilities and gave us a hope for the future. It is believed that 3D Printing Technology or Additive Manufacturing (AM) has the vast potential to become one of these technologies. There is a lot of coverage on 3D Printing Technology across many television channels, newspapers and online resources. What really is this 3D printing that some have claimed will put an end to traditional manufacturing as we know it, revolutionize design and impose geopolitical, economic, social, demographic and environmental and security implications to our everyday lives? The most basic, differentiating principle behind 3D Printing Technology is that it is an Additive Manufacturing process. And this is indeed the key because 3D Printing Technology is a radically different manufacturing method based on advanced technology that builds up parts, additively, in layers at the sub mm scale. This is fundamentally different from any existing traditional manufacturing techniques. Traditional manufacturing process has evolved a lot over time from hand based manufacturing to the automated processes such as Machining, Casting, Forming, Drilling, and Molding. Yet these technologies all demand subtracting material from a larger block – whether to achieve the end product itself or to produce a tool for casting or molding processes — and this is a serious limitation within the overall manufacturing process. For many applications traditional design and production processes impose a number of unacceptable constraints, including the expensive tooling, fixtures and the need for assembly for complex parts. In addition, the subtractive manufacturing processes, such as machining can result in up to 90% of the original block of material being wasted. In contrast, 3D Printing process can create objects directly by adding material layer by layer in a variety of ways, depending on the technology used. Simplifying the ideology behind 3D Printing, for anyone that is still trying to understand the concept, it could be likened to the process of building something with Lego blocks automatically.

Industries using 3D Printing Technology





Aerospace

The aerospace industry is increasingly adopting 3D Printing Technology or Additive Manufacturing Technology or Rapid Prototyping Technologies to develop aircraft parts to trim down manufacturing costs. In this industry, significant applications of 3D Printing Technology are manufacturing complex engine parts and wing components, space manufacturing, on-demand, low inventory, jigs and fixtures, surrogates, design validation, mounting brackets, and many more.

Defense

Design faster when time is critical. When customized equipment is vital, and deadlines are non-negotiable, 3D printing technology gives government, military, and defense manufacturers the freedom to design a single end-use part, quickly create low-volume tooling, or build complex, precise prototypes. Functional testing, production within timelines, customized gears and products, and mock-up models to study will aid the defense industry.

Mechanical and Automotive

Automotive engineers and designers can work more interactively, test more thoroughly, and move confidently into production by building durable concept models, prototypes, tooling, and low-volume end-use parts in-house. In this industry, significant applications of 3D Printing Technology are replacing expensive CNC production processes, creating better assembly tools, testing and identifying design problems, and product development.



Die and Mould Manufacturing

3D Printing Technology offers the world's most advanced method of printing molds, allowing mold designers to print production molds in one or two days instead of days and weeks as in the traditional practice. 3D Printing Technology helps mold makers when complex geometry would make standard tooling difficult, low quantities are needed, or design changes are likely. They can now save production time; design changes are more cost-effective and deliver accurate molds on time.

Medical

3D Printing Technology offers the medical world the most advanced solutions, allowing medical product manufacturers, doctors, and researchers to create parts for patient care and advanced experimental work. Technology offers Bio-compatible Materials for Rapid Prototyping of medical and dental products, including dental delivery devices, orthopedic surgical guides, and hearing aids. The medical materials offer excellent visualization and outstanding dimensional stability.

Dental

3D Printing Technology is the future of orthodontics. 3D Printing can help Dentists digitalize their workflow, provide fast ROI for labs and clinics of any size, and help in surgical planning and preparation. Combining oral scanning, CAD/CAM design, and 3D Printing, dental labs can accurately and rapidly produce crowns, bridges, stone models, and a range of orthodontic appliances.



Pharmaceutical Industry

3D Printing Technology in pharmaceutical drug delivery has been anticipated to excel tremendously in personalized medicines. We have reached an era in pharmacy practice and medicine whereby "one size does not fit all" since medication must be tailored to individual patients' needs while considering differences in genetic profiles, age, race, gender, epigenetic and environmental factors. Also, there are situations where the treatment regimens have to be customized to improve the patient's adherence to treatment.

Consumer Products

3D Printing Technology makes a great product better. Product designers can now check and validate the aesthetic as well as functional testing pre-production, create life-like prototypes, and can also experiment with different materials before finalizing. Additive Manufacturing has created new possibilities in the consumer goods market through functional prototyping, design, tooling, and even series-part production. Additive Manufacturing applications in the consumer product industry are growing and adapting to the specific nature and constraints of a rapidly expanding sector with an ever-increasing need to reduce development time.

Jewelry Industry

Physical models created by 3D printers make it possible for the design team to review the concepts. This guarantees the manufacturer gets better feedback, resulting in a superior product. Additionally, 3D Printing Technology allows manufacturers to realize real-time teamwork on a global scale. Recently, 3D Printing has increased drastically, with many industries adopting this technology. The jewelry industry was among the first to utilize 3D Printing in their 'investment casting' procedure. Instead of metal printers, they use wax here.



Foot ware Industry

As for footwear, 3D Printing Technology used by sneaker brands Nike, Adidas, New Balance, and Reebok, to name a few, major brands have tried it out with varying degrees of success. One of the primary interests of the technology lies in the possibilities of customization: take Adidas Futurecraft. Unlike traditional techniques, 3D Printing would allow Adidas to address each customer's specific needs by printing each sole according to the foot of its customers. Also, the 3D Printed outsole will enable you to configure its structure to maximize the performance of the final shoe: accentuate more pressurized areas and make points with low pressure more flexible.

Fashion Design

In recent years, the use of 3D Printing Technology in the fashion industry has increased during the creative process. Additive Manufacturing gives fashion a wide range of possibilities for prototyping or production and fascinates some designers. Additive Manufacturing is now easily applied to rigid creations and geometric shapes rather than flexible garments. 3D Printing can significantly expand creative possibilities for fashion design. Indeed, it makes it possible to create shapes without molds and thus produce elements of an extreme intricacy that one could not reach otherwise. NASA is also working on printing a 3D mesh coat for cosmonauts.

Ceramic Industry

The increasing demand for ceramics in the healthcare industry will provide a lucrative opportunity for expanding the ceramic 3D Printing Technology market. Ceramics are mainly used in tiles, pottery, crockery, sanitary utility items, and dental sectors for dental restoration applications, including crowns, bridges, and veneers. Developments in ceramic materials, including zirconia, can be used as monolithic materials that eliminate porcelain chipping without losing the physical stability of materials, thereby offering a broader application in dental resonation. Ceramics are widely used in several applications, including biomedical, aerospace, automotive, and electronics engineering, owing to high mechanical strength & hardness and good thermal and chemical stability. Developments in ceramic 3D Printing enable easy fabrication of complex geometries and thereby escalate the product demand.




Culinary/Food Industry

3D Food Printing Technology offers a range of potential benefits. It can be healthy and good for the environment because it can convert alternative ingredients, such as proteins from algae, beet leaves, or insects, into tasty products. It also opens the door to food customization and tunes into individual needs and preferences. 3D Food Printing is poised to have a massive impact on the global economy, closing the gap between small and large-scale businesses while giving consumers a significant degree of freedom in choosing the food they want. Many experts believe that global food shortage challenges can be effectively addressed by 3D Printing food. Even though the technology is complex and expensive, 3D Printers can soon become ubiquitous as a household microwave oven.

Civil, Architecture, and Interior Design

Seeing is believing. 3D Printing Technology helps architectural firms seize opportunities by creating complex, durable models in-house, directly from CAD data. In this industry, 3D Printing Technology helps client visualize and understand their designs better, reduce the time spent creating models, and build a design library for reusable designs. At the same time, Construction 3D Printers are taking the world into a modern era where low-cost housing and construction are possible.

Commercial products

Commercial product manufacturers need the flexibility to make great products in small, medium, or large volumes. Digital manufacturing solutions often differ between turning down potential businesses and cost-effectively delivering and serving a niche need. Timing can be as important as the idea itself. In the competitive consumer products field, in-house 3D Printing enhances collaboration, shrinks time to market, and helps keep new devices under wraps until launch.




Electronics

In electronics, requirements of attractive design, small form factor, and high production efficiency make designing truly successful consumer electronics a real challenge. 3D Printing Technology helps developers check the fit of small and large components during the validation stage. Creating workable real-time prototypes helps them understand their product better and can also experiment with various materials.

Education

Introducing new technologies fires up a kid's imagination and inspires them to learn without limits. A structured 3D Printing Technology curriculum adds a new dimension to the classroom by integrating making into learning. Curriculum-supported teaching tools make learning fun and teach science, technology, engineering, and math concepts easy. Learning 3D Printing Technology enriches an effective interactive learning method, boosts their career opportunities, and can give them an advantage in the employment market. And many more
